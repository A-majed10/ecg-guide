01 Electrophysiology

What the ink actually represents

The ECG is not a picture of the heart. It is a voltage-versus-time plot of the summed electrical field that millions of myocytes generate as they depolarise and repolarise in sequence. Get this layer right and everything downstream — axis, blocks, infarcts — becomes deduction rather than recall.

The single cell

A resting ventricular myocyte sits at roughly −90 mV, held there by K⁺ gradients. An arriving wavefront pushes it past threshold and it fires a stereotyped action potential:

Action potential phases

  • Phase 0 — upstroke. Voltage-gated Na⁺ channels open; the cell depolarises in <2 ms. This near-synchronous firing across the ventricle is what writes the QRS.
  • Phase 1 — notch. Transient outward K⁺ current; a brief partial repolarisation.
  • Phase 2 — plateau. Inward Ca²⁺ balances outward K⁺. Net membrane current is near zero, so the surface tracing is isoelectric — this is the ST segment.
  • Phase 3 — repolarisation. Ca²⁺ channels close, K⁺ efflux dominates and the cell returns to rest. This writes the T wave.
  • Phase 4 — rest. The diastolic baseline. In pacemaker cells this phase is unstable and drifts upward (the funny current, If) — that automaticity is what makes the SA node the default rhythm generator.

From one cell to the body surface

A single firing cell is a moving dipole — a separation of charge with direction and magnitude, i.e. a vector. An electrode records the projection of that vector onto its own axis. One rule does most of the work:

The rule everything rests on

A wave of depolarisation moving toward a recording (positive) electrode inscribes an upward deflection; moving away, a downward one; perpendicular, a biphasic or flat one. The taller the deflection, the more the net vector points at that lead.

The whole 12-lead is this rule applied from twelve vantage points to the same travelling wavefront. Because the heart's mass is left-sided and depolarises overall toward the left and slightly inferior, the mean QRS vector normally points to about +60° — which is why lead II, sitting at +60°, shows a tall upright QRS in almost everyone.

Why the normal T wave points the same way as the QRS

Depolarisation sweeps endocardium→epicardium, but repolarisation runs epicardium→endocardium (the epicardium, under less pressure, recovers first). A wave of recovery moving away from the electrode reads positive — the same polarity as depolarisation moving toward it. Two reversals cancel, so the T is normally concordant with the QRS. When something forces repolarisation to run the wrong way (ischaemia, strain, BBB), the T flips. That is the mechanistic basis of most T-wave abnormalities.

The conduction system sets the timing

  • SA node (high right atrium) fires; the impulse spreads cell-to-cell across the atria → the P wave.
  • AV node deliberately delays conduction (~100 ms). This decremental gate protects the ventricles from fast atrial rates and creates the flat PR segment; nodal tissue is the slow, vulnerable link exploited by Wenckebach and by AV-nodal re-entry.
  • His–Purkinje system — bundle of His → right and left bundle branches → Purkinje fibres — conducts fast, firing the ventricular mass almost simultaneously. That synchrony is why a normal QRS is narrow (<120 ms). Disease here doesn't slow conduction so much as reroute it, widening and reshaping the QRS in patterns you can read off directly.

02 The twelve leads

Twelve vantage points, one event

A “lead” is a defined viewing axis, not a wire. The twelve standard leads split into two planes: six limb leads view the heart in the frontal (coronal) plane, six precordial leads in the horizontal (transverse) plane. Localising any finding means asking which leads — which directions — are involved.

Frontal plane — the limb leads

Bipolar (Einthoven)

  • Lead I = left arm minus right arm, axis 0° (points to the patient's left).
  • Lead II = left leg minus right arm, axis +60°.
  • Lead III = left leg minus left arm, axis +120°.
  • Einthoven's law: II = I + III at every instant. If the limb leads don't obey it, suspect lead misplacement.

Augmented (Goldberger)

  • aVR at −150°, aVL at −30°, aVF at +90°.
  • aVR points at the cavity from the right shoulder, so normal complexes in aVR are inverted — it looks at the heart “from behind the electrical activity.” That contrarian view makes aVR uniquely useful (global ischaemia, left-main disease, poisoning).

Stacked through a common centre, these six axes tile the frontal plane every 30° — the hexaxial reference we use for axis.

Horizontal plane — the precordial leads

  • V1–V2 sit over the right ventricle and interventricular septum (anteroseptal).
  • V3–V4 over the anterior wall / apex.
  • V5–V6 over the lateral wall.
  • Moving V1→V6, the R wave should grow and the S wave shrink as the leads swing from facing the rightward septal forces to facing the dominant leftward LV forces — normal R-wave progression, with the transition (R = S) around V3–V4.

Contiguous territories

Localisation is the whole game in ischaemia. Group leads by the wall and vessel they represent:

Calibration — check it before you read

  • Standard paper speed 25 mm/s: one small (1 mm) box = 40 ms; one large (5 mm) box = 200 ms.
  • Standard gain 10 mm/mV: 1 mm = 0.1 mV; the square calibration pulse should be 10 mm tall.
  • Half-standard gain (pulse 5 mm tall) halves every amplitude — miss it and you'll under-call LVH or over-call low voltage. Double speed (50 mm/s) stretches intervals. Read the calibration marker first, every time.
Pearl

If a finding appears in only one lead, distrust it — it is more likely artefact or a single-lead quirk than a real, localised electrical event. Real pathology respects anatomy and shows up in a contiguous group.

03 A systematic read

The same nine steps, every tracing

The expensive errors in ECG reading are almost never “I didn't know the pattern.” They are anchoring on the first abnormality and missing a second, quieter one. A fixed sequence is the cheap insurance against that.

The order

  1. Demographics & calibration — right patient, speed and gain, and the prior ECG if one exists. Comparison changes management more often than any single feature.
  2. Rate — ventricular and, where they differ, atrial.
  3. Rhythm — is there a P before every QRS and a QRS after every P, regular, at a sensible rate from a sensible focus?
  4. Axis — frontal-plane QRS direction.
  5. Intervals — PR, QRS width, QT/QTc.
  6. P-wave morphology — atrial enlargement.
  7. QRS morphology — voltage, Q waves, R-wave progression, bundle branch patterns.
  8. ST segments & T waves — elevation, depression, inversion, by territory.
  9. The targeted question — now overlay the clinical context. Chest pain, syncope, palpitation, electrolyte risk and hyperkalaemia, drug effect each direct a final focused look.
Why finish, don't stop

An obvious inferior STEMI can sit on the same tracing as a subtle Mobitz II or a long QT that changes what you do next. Reading to the end of the sequence regardless of how striking step 7 was is what separates a complete read from a lucky one.

04 Rate

Counting, three ways

Normal sinus rate is 60–100 bpm; below is bradycardia, above is tachycardia. Pick the counting method to fit the rhythm's regularity.

Regular rhythms

  • 300 rule — divide 300 by the number of large boxes between two R waves: 1 box = 300, 2 = 150, 3 = 100, 4 = 75, 5 = 60, 6 = 50. Fast and good enough at the bedside.
  • 1500 rule — for precision, divide 1500 by the number of small boxes between R waves. Use it when the rate matters (e.g. distinguishing 145 from 165).

Irregular rhythms

  • 6-second rule — count the QRS complexes in a 6-second strip (30 large boxes) and multiply by 10. The only honest method when R–R intervals vary, as in atrial fibrillation.
Atrial ≠ ventricular when they're uncoupled

In flutter, complete heart block, or any high-grade AV block, count the atrial (P-to-P) and ventricular (R-to-R) rates separately. A ventricular rate parked at almost exactly 150 should make you look hard for flutter waves at 300 with 2:1 conduction.

05 Rhythm

Who is driving, and is the conduction intact

Rhythm analysis is one organising question asked carefully: is there a P wave before every QRS, a QRS after every P, a constant relationship between them, and does the whole thing come from a sensible focus at a sensible rate?

Normal sinus rhythm

  • A P wave before every QRS and a QRS after every P.
  • P upright in II and inverted in aVR — the signature of a wavefront leaving the high right atrium and travelling down-and-left, toward II and away from aVR.
  • Constant, normal PR (120–200 ms) and a rate of 60–100.

Lose any one of these and you name what's missing: no consistent P (atrial fibrillation), P axis wrong (ectopic atrial or junctional focus), P–QRS relationship variable (AV block or dissociation).

A reproducible approach to any rhythm

Three axes of classification

  • Rate — brady (<60), normal, or tachy (>100).
  • Regularity — regular, regularly irregular (patterned, e.g. grouped beating), or irregularly irregular (AF, MAT, varying-conduction flutter).
  • QRS width — narrow (<120 ms → supraventricular, conducting through the His–Purkinje system) or wide (≥120 ms → ventricular origin, bundle branch block, or pre-excitation).

Those three answers plus the P-wave question place almost every rhythm before you've memorised a single named arrhythmia.

Sinus arrhythmia and ectopy

  • Sinus arrhythmia — phasic R–R variation with respiration (rate rises on inspiration). Normal, especially in the young; P morphology and PR stay constant.
  • PAC — a premature, abnormally shaped P from an ectopic atrial focus; narrow QRS, usually an incomplete (non-compensatory) pause.
  • PVC — a premature, wide, bizarre QRS with no preceding P, the T pointing opposite the QRS, typically followed by a full compensatory pause (the sinus node, undisturbed, marches through).
Premature vs escape — same shape, opposite meaning

A wide ventricular complex that comes early is a PVC (irritable focus). The identical-looking complex that comes late, after a pause, is a ventricular escape beat — a safety mechanism, not an irritant. Timing relative to the expected beat tells you which, and whether to worry about ectopy or about why the higher pacemaker failed.

06 Axis

The mean direction of ventricular depolarisation

The QRS axis is the net frontal-plane vector of ventricular activation. Normal is roughly −30° to +90°. It is worth a few seconds on every tracing because a shift is often the first or only clue to a fascicular block, an old infarct, or a strained right heart.

Determining it in two moves

  1. Quadrant by I and aVF. Look at the net QRS (taller of R vs S) in each:
    • I up, aVF up → normal (0 to +90).
    • I up, aVF down → left axis (possible — confirm with II).
    • I down, aVF up → right axis.
    • I down, aVF down → extreme / northwest axis.
  2. Refine with lead II and the isoelectric lead. If II is net positive, the axis is above −30° (still normal); if II is net negative, true left axis deviation. The axis points roughly perpendicular to whichever limb lead has the most equiphasic (isoelectric) QRS.

Deviations and what causes them

Left axis deviation (−30° to −90°)

  • Left anterior fascicular block (the most common cause).
  • Inferior MI (loss of inferior forces pulls the vector up-and-left).
  • LVH (sometimes), some pre-excitation, mechanical shift (pregnancy, ascites), paced/LBBB.

Right axis deviation (+90° to +180°)

  • Right ventricular hypertrophy / cor pulmonale, acute right-heart strain (PE).
  • Left posterior fascicular block (a diagnosis of exclusion).
  • Lateral MI, some pre-excitation; normal in children and tall thin adults.
The isoelectric shortcut

Find the limb lead whose QRS is most balanced between up and down. The mean axis is ~90° away from it, pointing toward whichever lead the QRS is most upright in. This is faster and more accurate than the quadrant method once it clicks — and it's the same logic the hexaxial diagram in section 2 makes visual.

07 Waves & intervals

Anatomy of a single complex

Each deflection has a generator, a normal range, and a short list of ways it deviates. Learn the complex component by component and most “patterns” later are just combinations you can name.

The P wave — atrial depolarisation

Right atrium depolarises first (the early half of the P), then the left (the late half). Normal P is <120 ms wide and <2.5 mm tall, best judged in II and V1.

  • Right atrial enlargement — “P pulmonale.” Tall (≥2.5 mm), peaked P in II; the enlarged right atrium adds amplitude to the early component.
  • Left atrial enlargement — “P mitrale.” Broad, notched (≥120 ms) P in II and a deep, wide terminal negative deflection in V1; the delayed left-atrial component drags out the late portion.

The PR interval — AV transit time

  • Measured from P onset to QRS onset; normal 120–200 ms, most of it the deliberate AV-nodal delay.
  • Long PR (>200 ms) → first-degree AV block (section 8).
  • Short PR (<120 ms) → an accessory pathway pre-exciting the ventricle (look for a delta wave) or a low-atrial/junctional focus starting closer to the ventricle.

The QRS — ventricular depolarisation

Reading the components

  • A Q is any initial downward deflection, R any upward, S the downward after an R. Normal duration <120 ms.
  • The septal vector. The septum depolarises first and left-to-right, producing a small septal q in left-sided leads (I, aVL, V5–V6) and a small initial r in V1. These tiny normal q's are why losing them, or growing pathological ones, is meaningful.
  • R-wave progression. R grows and S shrinks V1→V6 as the leads come to face the dominant LV. Poor progression suggests anteroseptal scar, LVH, or lead placement.
Pathological Q wave

A Q is pathological when it is ≥40 ms wide or >25% of the R-wave height in that lead. Mechanism: infarcted muscle is electrically silent, so the only vector left in that region points away — the electrode over dead myocardium now “sees through” to forces moving away and writes a deep initial negativity. Septal q's are narrow and shallow; infarct Q's are broad and deep.

ST segment, T wave, QT, U wave

  • ST segment — the plateau (phase 2), normally isoelectric. The J point (QRS–ST junction) is the reference for measuring elevation or depression. Detail in sections 10–11.
  • T wave — repolarisation (phase 3); normally asymmetric (slow up, faster down) and concordant with the QRS. Inversion follows ischaemia, strain, BBB/pacing (secondary), or CNS catastrophe.
  • QT interval — total ventricular electrical activity, QRS onset to T end. It shortens as heart rate rises, so rate-correct it: Bazett QTc = QT / √RR. Normal QTc <440 ms (men) / <460 ms (women).
  • U wave — a small deflection after the T (late Purkinje repolarisation); prominent in hypokalaemia and bradycardia.
Measure the QT properly

Use the lead with the longest QT (often II or V5–V6), define T-end by the tangent method (steepest downslope extended to baseline), and exclude any U wave. A genuinely long QTc widens the window for an R-on-T beat to trigger Torsades de pointes — the reason QT prolongation by drugs, electrolytes, or congenital channelopathy is a can't-miss.

08 Conduction blocks

Where the impulse stalls or detours

Two families: AV blocks (a problem getting from atria to ventricles, read off the P–QRS relationship) and intraventricular blocks (a problem distributing within the ventricles, read off QRS width and shape). The clinical weight of a block depends almost entirely on where it sits.

AV block — first degree

Every P conducts, but slowly: PR fixed and >200 ms. It is a description, not a dropped-beat rhythm, and is usually benign.

AV block — second degree

Mobitz I (Wenckebach)

  • Progressive PR lengthening until a P fails to conduct (a dropped QRS), then the cycle resets. R–R intervals paradoxically shorten before the dropped beat.
  • Site: AV node. Decremental nodal tissue fatiguing — usually benign, often vagal, drug-induced, or inferior-MI related; typically responds to atropine.

Mobitz II

  • PR constant, then a sudden dropped QRS with no warning. Often a fixed conduction ratio (2:1, 3:1).
  • Site: infranodal (His–Purkinje). An all-or-none diseased distal system — unstable, prone to progression to complete block, and a pacemaker indication.
The distinction that changes management

Mobitz I is a tired node; Mobitz II is a failing wire. Lengthening-then-drop is benign and watched; constant-then-drop is dangerous and paced. In a 2:1 block you can't see PR trend across consecutive conducted beats — use width of the conducted QRS (narrow favours nodal/Mobitz I, wide favours infranodal/Mobitz II) and response to vagal/atropine manoeuvres to infer the site.

AV block — third degree (complete)

  • Complete AV dissociation: P–P regular and R–R regular, but unrelated — atria and ventricles are driven by separate pacemakers.
  • The escape rate and QRS width localise the block: a narrow QRS at 40–60 is a junctional escape (block high, relatively stable); a wide QRS at 20–40 is a ventricular escape (block low, slow, unreliable — pace).

Bundle branch block — the mechanism

Block one bundle and that ventricle can no longer be activated through the fast Purkinje network; it must be reached late, by slow muscle-to-muscle spread from the other side. That delay widens the QRS to ≥120 ms and throws the terminal forces toward the late-activating ventricle — which is exactly what the lead patterns show.

Right bundle branch block

  • Late, unopposed RV depolarisation points rightward and anteriorly → a terminal R′ in V1 (rSR′, “rabbit ears”) and a wide slurred S in the left leads I and V6.
  • Appropriate (secondary) ST–T discordance in V1–V3. RBBB does not hide ischaemia the way LBBB does.

Left bundle branch block

  • The whole LV is activated late and abnormally → a broad monophasic R in I, aVL, V5–V6 (often notched) with loss of the septal q, and a deep QS or rS in V1.
  • Marked secondary ST–T discordance (ST/T opposite the main QRS deflection) is expected — which is why LBBB masks acute ischaemia and forces the Sgarbossa approach (section 10).
Which V1 to read — the mechanism, not a mnemonic

In V1 the terminal QRS direction names the block: terminal forces toward V1 (an upright R′) = RBBB, because the late ventricle is the anterior right one; terminal forces away (a deep QS) = LBBB, because the late ventricle is the leftward one. The old “WiLLiaM MaRRoW” pattern is just this vector logic memorised backwards.

Fascicular and combined blocks

  • Left anterior fascicular block (LAFB): left axis deviation (more negative than −45°) with qR in aVL and rS in the inferior leads, QRS only mildly widened. The commonest cause of marked left axis.
  • Left posterior fascicular block (LPFB): right axis deviation with rS in I/aVL and qR inferiorly — rare, and a diagnosis of exclusion (rule out RVH, lateral MI, PE first).
  • Bifascicular block: RBBB + a fascicular block (e.g. RBBB + LAFB). Trifascicular implies bifascicular block plus delay in the remaining fascicle (often a long PR) — a system running on one fascicle.
When a block needs a pacemaker

Mobitz II, complete heart block, and a wide/slow ventricular escape are pacing indications. So is new bifascicular or trifascicular block with syncope — the patient may be having intermittent complete block between tracings. The asymptomatic chronic block is watched; the symptomatic or distal one is paced.

09 Chamber enlargement

More muscle, or stretched walls

Hypertrophy adds voltage; dilatation and pressure load reshape repolarisation (“strain”). The atrial patterns live in the P wave (section 7); here the ventricles.

Left ventricular hypertrophy

Voltage criteria (any one suggests LVH)

  • Sokolow–Lyon: S in V1 + R in V5 or V6 ≥35 mm.
  • Cornell: R in aVL + S in V3 >28 mm (men) / >20 mm (women).
  • Limb lead: R in aVL ≥11 mm.
  • Supportive: left axis, LAE, and a “strain” pattern — down-sloping ST depression with asymmetric T inversion in the lateral leads (I, aVL, V5–V6).
Specific, not sensitive

Voltage criteria are heavily modified by body habitus — tall amplitudes are common in thin young patients without disease, and a thick chest wall hides real LVH. Treat them as a flag to integrate with axis, LAE, strain, and the echo, not as a standalone diagnosis. LVH with strain also lowers the bar for over-calling ischaemic ST changes.

Right ventricular hypertrophy

  • The normally dominated RV becomes large enough to throw forces rightward and anteriorly: dominant R in V1 (R/S > 1), right axis deviation, and a deep S persisting into V5–V6.
  • Often with right atrial enlargement and a right precordial (V1–V3) strain pattern. Causes: pulmonary hypertension, chronic lung disease, pulmonary stenosis, and (acutely) the right-heart strain of a large PE.

10 Ischaemia & infarction

Reading coronary occlusion off the surface

Two things to extract: is there acute injury, and which territory and vessel. Both come from applying the vector rule to ST segments, T waves and Q waves — with the prior ECG and the clinical story alongside.

The evolution of a transmural infarct

Time stamps the ECG. A complete epicardial occlusion marches through a recognisable sequence, and the stage you catch tells you how old the event is:

  • Hyperacute T (minutes) — tall, broad, symmetric T waves; the earliest and most missed sign.
  • ST elevation (minutes–hours) — convex/straightened ST rising from the J point; the injury current of transmural ischaemia.
  • Pathological Q (hours–days) — necrotic muscle goes electrically silent; a deep, wide Q appears.
  • T-wave inversion and gradual ST normalisation (days–weeks). Persistent ST elevation beyond weeks suggests an aneurysm.

STEMI thresholds and reciprocal change

J-point elevation that meets STEMI criteria

  • ≥1 mm in two contiguous limb or precordial leads, except
  • V2–V3: ≥2 mm (men ≥40), ≥2.5 mm (men <40), ≥1.5 mm (women).
  • New (or presumed new) LBBB with an ischaemic presentation is managed as a STEMI-equivalent.
Reciprocal change is the same vector, viewed from the opposite side

An injury vector that elevates ST in the leads facing it will depress ST in the leads looking from the other side. Reciprocal depression isn't a second problem — it's confirmation, and it raises specificity. Inferior STEMI (II, III, aVF) reciprocates in aVL; high lateral STEMI reciprocates inferiorly.

Two territories you must go looking for

Posterior MI

  • No anterior lead sits over the posterior wall, so it shows up in reverse in V1–V3: horizontal ST depression, tall broad R waves, and upright T — the mirror of posterior Q/STE/TWI.
  • Confirm by placing posterior leads V7–V9, where you'll see the true ST elevation. Frequently accompanies inferior or lateral infarcts.

Right ventricular MI

  • Suspect with any inferior STEMI; confirm with right-sided lead V4R (ST elevation there).
  • These patients are preload-dependent — nitrates can cause profound hypotension. Treat with fluids, avoid venodilators.

Non-ST-elevation ischaemia (subendocardial)

  • Partial-thickness ischaemia produces ST depression and/or T-wave inversion rather than elevation; the injury vector points to the subendocardium, away from the surface leads.
  • Dynamic changes that track with pain, and new deep symmetric T inversions, carry weight — especially in the LAD territory (see Wellens, section 11).

LBBB / paced rhythm — Sgarbossa

Because LBBB and pacing impose their own discordant ST–T, you can't use ordinary elevation thresholds. Sgarbossa criteria find infarction through the noise:

  • Concordant ST elevation ≥1 mm (ST in the same direction as the QRS) — most specific.
  • Concordant ST depression ≥1 mm in V1–V3.
  • Excessively discordant ST elevation (Smith-modified: ST/S ratio ≤ −0.25).

Not every ST elevation is a STEMI

Differential of ST elevation — and how to tell them apart
  • Benign early repolarisation — concave ST, notched J point, diffuse, stable; young and well.
  • Pericarditis — diffuse concave STE + PR depression, non-territorial, no reciprocal change (except aVR).
  • LVH / LBBB — elevation is discordant and proportional to large QRS voltages.
  • Brugada — coved STE confined to V1–V2.
  • Ventricular aneurysm — persistent STE with well-formed Q waves, weeks after an old infarct.
  • Hyperkalaemia — STE with peaked T's and a widening QRS.

The discriminators are the company it keeps: shape (concave vs convex), distribution (territorial vs diffuse), reciprocal change, evolution on serial tracings, and the prior ECG.

11 STEMI equivalents

Acute occlusion without the textbook elevation

A meaningful fraction of acutely occluded arteries never meet millimetre STEMI criteria. These high-risk patterns are how you catch “occlusion MI” that a pure ST-elevation rule would discharge.

Wellens' syndrome — critical proximal LAD

  • Deeply abnormal T waves in V2–V3, classically appearing after pain has resolved, with preserved R waves and no significant Q's or elevation.
  • Type A: biphasic T (up then down). Type B: deep, symmetric T inversion.
  • It signals a critically stenosed LAD with impending anterior infarction — refer for angiography, not a stress test (provocation can precipitate the infarct).

de Winter pattern — LAD occlusion equivalent

  • Up-sloping ST depression at the J point in the precordial leads rising into tall, symmetric T waves, often with slight aVR elevation. Static (doesn't evolve through classic STE), and present in up to ~2% of LAD occlusions.
  • Treat as a STEMI: it represents acute LAD occlusion.

aVR elevation with diffuse ST depression

  • ST elevation in aVR > V1 with widespread ST depression (often ≥6 leads) points to left-main or proximal triple-vessel subendocardial ischaemia — global supply–demand failure rather than a single occluded wall.
  • High-risk: these patients are sick, and the lesion is proximal. (The same aVR pattern appears in severe diffuse ischaemia from any cause, including post-arrest and poisoning.)

Hyperacute T waves

  • Broad, tall, symmetric T waves are the earliest sign of occlusion — before ST elevation declares itself. On a single ECG they can be subtle; on serial tracings they grow, then give way to elevation.
  • Distinguish from the peaked, narrow-based T of hyperkalaemia (section 14): hyperacute T's are broad-based and bulky; hyperkalaemic T's are tall, narrow and tented.
The unifying point

“STEMI” is a millimetre rule layered on top of the real question, which is acute coronary occlusion. Wellens, de Winter, posterior ST depression, and the aVR pattern are occlusions that don't fit the rule. Serial ECGs and comparison with priors are how you keep catching them.

12 Tachyarrhythmias

Fast rhythms, sorted by two questions

Width and regularity organise almost every tachycardia: narrow vs wide QRS (supraventricular vs ventricular/aberrant) and regular vs irregular. Add the P-wave relationship and you can place the rhythm and choose a safe move.

Narrow-complex — regular

  • Sinus tachycardia — normal P axis, rate usually <150 and driven by a cause (pain, fever, hypovolaemia, PE); it has a context.
  • AVNRT — the commonest paroxysmal SVT; a re-entry circuit inside the AV node. Regular, abrupt onset, 150–200; retrograde P buried in or just after the QRS (pseudo-S′ in inferior leads, pseudo-r′ in V1).
  • Orthodromic AVRT — re-entry down the node and back up an accessory pathway; retrograde P typically after the QRS.
  • Atrial flutter — sawtooth atrial activity at ~300 with fixed conduction; a ventricular rate of ~150 is flutter with 2:1 block until proven otherwise.
Adenosine as a diagnostic, not just a treatment

In a regular narrow-complex tachycardia, transient AV-nodal block breaks node-dependent circuits (AVNRT, orthodromic AVRT) and, when it doesn't terminate the rhythm, unmasks the atrial activity — flutter waves or atrial tachycardia march out while the ventricles pause. Either way you learn the mechanism.

Narrow-complex — irregular

  • Atrial fibrillation — no organised P waves, irregularly irregular ventricular response. The irregularity is the diagnosis.
  • Multifocal atrial tachycardia (MAT) — ≥3 distinct P morphologies with varying PR and P–P intervals; classically in severe COPD.
  • Flutter with variable block — sawtooth baseline, irregular ventricular response.

Wide-complex tachycardia — assume VT

A wide regular tachycardia is ventricular tachycardia until proven otherwise, especially with any history of structural heart disease or prior MI. Misclassifying VT as SVT and giving a nodal blocker can be fatal.

Features that favour VT

  • AV dissociation — independent P waves marching through — and especially capture or fusion beats, which prove a ventricular driver. Near-pathognomonic.
  • Very wide QRS (>140 ms RBBB-like / >160 ms LBBB-like), extreme/northwest axis, and precordial concordance (all V leads same polarity).
  • Morphology not fitting a typical RBBB/LBBB template.

Polymorphic VT and VF

  • Torsades de pointes — polymorphic VT on a long QT, the axis twisting around the baseline. Treat with IV magnesium and correct the QT cause.
  • Ventricular fibrillation — disorganised, no complexes: a shockable arrest rhythm.

Pre-excitation (WPW)

  • At baseline: short PR, a delta wave, and a wide QRS — an accessory pathway pre-exciting the ventricle.
  • It enables re-entrant AVRT, and dangerously, if atrial fibrillation develops, the pathway can conduct rapidly to the ventricle — an irregular, wide, very fast tachycardia.
AF in WPW — do not block the node

Giving AV-nodal blockers (adenosine, verapamil, diltiazem, digoxin, β-blockers) in pre-excited AF removes the node's braking and forces more impulses down the fast accessory pathway — it can accelerate to ventricular fibrillation. Use procainamide (or electrical cardioversion if unstable). Irregular + wide + very fast = think pre-excited AF.

13 Bradyarrhythmias

Slow rhythms and the escape hierarchy

Bradycardia is a rate <60. The questions are where the failure is — impulse generation (SA node), conduction (AV block, covered in section 8) — and whether a backup pacemaker has stepped in.

Sinus node problems

  • Sinus bradycardia — normal P morphology, slow rate. Often physiological (athletes, sleep, vagal tone) or due to drugs (β-blockers, calcium-channel blockers), hypothyroidism, raised intracranial pressure, hypothermia, or inferior MI affecting the SA nodal artery.
  • Sinus pause / arrest — the SA node transiently fails to fire; a flat gap until an escape or the next sinus beat.
  • Sick sinus syndrome — chronic SA node dysfunction, often alternating brady and atrial tachyarrhythmias (tachy–brady syndrome); a common reason for pacing in the elderly.

Escape rhythms — the failsafe

When a higher pacemaker fails, a lower one takes over at its own intrinsic rate. The escape's rate and width tell you how far down the system the rescue is coming from:

  • Junctional escape — 40–60 bpm, narrow QRS, with absent or retrograde (inverted-in-II) P waves. A relatively reliable backup near the AV node.
  • Ventricular escape — 20–40 bpm, wide QRS. A last-ditch pacemaker — slow and unstable, and the reason a wide, slow escape in complete heart block needs pacing.
Read escape as information, not just rhythm

An escape beat is the system telling you the level of a conduction failure. Narrow and ~50 means the block is high and a dependable junctional pacemaker has it; wide and ~30 means the block is low and only ventricular tissue is left to drive — a far more precarious situation.

14 Systemic & metabolic

When the ECG reflects the whole patient

Electrolytes, drugs, temperature, channelopathies and the lungs all write on the ECG. Several of these are faster than the laboratory and some are the only warning you'll get.

Potassium

  • Hyperkalaemia climbs a predictable ladder: peaked, narrow-based (“tented”) T waves → P-wave flattening and PR prolongation → QRS widening → a sine wave as QRS and T merge → arrest. Trust the ECG over the number and treat (calcium to stabilise the membrane, then shift and remove K⁺).
  • Hypokalaemia — T-wave flattening, ST depression, and a prominent U wave that can fuse with the T, with QT prolongation and Torsades risk.

Calcium

  • Hypocalcaemia — lengthens the ST segment and so the QT (the T itself stays normal; the plateau drags out).
  • Hypercalcaemia — shortens the ST and the QT.

Digoxin

  • Effect (therapeutic) — down-sloping “scooped” ST depression (the sagging-moustache), flattened T's, shortened QT. A normal finding on the drug.
  • Toxicity — a different beast: increased automaticity plus slowed conduction, classically atrial tachycardia with AV block, junctional rhythms, and the near-specific bidirectional VT.

Temperature — the Osborn wave

In hypothermia, a positive deflection at the J point (the Osborn / J wave) grows as core temperature falls, alongside bradycardia, prolonged intervals, and shivering artefact.

Channelopathies and the long QT

  • Long QT — congenital, or acquired from drugs (many antiarrhythmics, antipsychotics, macrolides, antiemetics), hypokalaemia/hypomagnesaemia/hypocalcaemia, or bradycardia. The danger is Torsades; always rate-correct (QTc) and stack risk factors.
  • Brugada syndrome — a sodium channelopathy: coved (type 1) ST elevation in V1–V2 and a risk of sudden death; can be unmasked by fever or sodium-channel blockers.

Pericardium and effusion

  • Acute pericarditis — diffuse concave ST elevation with PR-segment depression (and reciprocal PR elevation / ST depression in aVR); evolves through T-wave inversion over days. The non-territorial spread is the key separator from STEMI.
  • Large effusion / tamponade — low voltage and electrical alternans (beat-to-beat QRS amplitude swing) as the heart swings in fluid; usually with sinus tachycardia.

The lungs — acute PE

  • The commonest ECG finding is plain sinus tachycardia. Signs of acute right-heart strain are less sensitive but more specific: S1Q3T3 (S in I, Q and T inversion in III), T-wave inversion in V1–V4, new right axis deviation, and incomplete/complete RBBB.
  • The ECG supports, but never excludes, the diagnosis.

Two cardiomyopathy signatures worth recognising

  • HCM — high LV voltages with deep, narrow “septal” Q waves; the apical variant produces giant precordial T-wave inversions.
  • ARVC — an epsilon wave (a small terminal notch after the QRS) and T-wave inversion in V1–V3, in a young patient with right-ventricular-origin arrhythmias.

15 Pacemakers

Reading a paced tracing

A paced ECG has a vocabulary of its own: the pacing spike and the morphology it produces. Knowing the normal paced pattern is what lets you recognise when a device is failing.

Spikes and morphology

  • A pacing spike is a sharp, narrow vertical deflection. An atrial spike is followed by a P; a ventricular spike by a wide QRS.
  • RV apical pacing activates the left ventricle late and from the right — producing an LBBB-like, wide QRS with a left/northwest axis. That's the expected look, not a second pathology.
  • Dual-chamber pacing shows a spike before the P and another before the QRS.

Modes, briefly

  • The code names chamber paced / sensed / response: VVI paces and senses the ventricle and inhibits on an intrinsic beat; DDD paces and senses both chambers, preserving AV synchrony.

Failure modes

  • Failure to capture — a spike with no following complex; the output didn't depolarise the myocardium (lead, threshold, battery, electrolytes).
  • Failure to sense (undersensing) — spikes appear despite adequate intrinsic beats, sometimes landing dangerously on a T wave.
  • Failure to output / oversensing — expected spikes are missing because the device wrongly sensed activity (e.g. myopotentials) and withheld pacing.
Spot failure by comparison

Read a paced ECG by asking whether every spike does its job (capture) and whether every spike belongs there (sensing). A spike without a complex is failure to capture; a complex that should have inhibited a spike but didn't is failure to sense. The normal paced morphology is your reference for both.

16 Pitfalls & checklist

Don't be fooled, then finish clean

The last skill is distrust applied in the right places: confirming the tracing is technically valid before believing what it shows, and separating noise from rhythm.

Lead misplacement and reversal

  • LA/RA reversal — lead I inverts (negative P and QRS in I), and aVR may look oddly normal/upright. A globally negative I with otherwise healthy complexes is reversal until proven otherwise — check that Einthoven's law (II = I + III) still holds.
  • Dextrocardia mimics arm-lead reversal but also shows reversed precordial progression (R waves shrink V1→V6) — that precordial pattern is how you tell true dextrocardia from a simple cable swap.
  • Precordial misplacement — a common cause of “poor R-wave progression” that vanishes on a repeat with correct placement.

Artefact versus arrhythmia

  • Tremor (Parkinsonian or shivering) and 50/60-Hz interference can fake atrial flutter, VT, even VF. The tells: the patient is well and perfusing, and the underlying QRS complexes often march through the noise at a normal rate — find them and the “arrhythmia” dissolves.
  • Always reconcile the rhythm on the monitor with the patient in front of you before treating a strip.
Treat the patient, not the tracing

No ECG finding outranks the clinical picture. A “VF” alarm on a chatting, well-perfused patient is artefact; subtle ST changes in someone with crushing chest pain are an occlusion until disproven. The tracing is one data point in a person.

The closing read-out

Run the same sequence on every tracing, out loud if it helps:

  1. Patient, date, calibration (25 mm/s, 10 mm/mV), and the prior ECG.
  2. Rate — ventricular and atrial.
  3. Rhythm — P before every QRS, regular, sensible focus.
  4. Axis.
  5. Intervals — PR, QRS, QTc.
  6. P morphology — atrial enlargement.
  7. QRS — voltage, Q waves, R-wave progression, bundle branch pattern.
  8. ST–T — elevation, depression, inversion, by territory, with reciprocals.
  9. The clinical question — the focused look the presentation demands.

Do this every time and the rare, quiet, dangerous finding gets the same attention as the loud, obvious one. That discipline — not pattern memorisation — is what reading the ECG actually is.

Read every tracing the same way, every time.
Rate → rhythm → axis → intervals → P / QRS / ST–T morphology → the specific question the clinical context is asking. Pattern recognition is the reward for vector reasoning, not a substitute for it — and the ECG is one test in a patient, never the patient.
